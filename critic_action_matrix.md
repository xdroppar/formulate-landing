## Agent A: Blocking Safety/Contraindication Sections (73)

These are YMYL liability issues. Each guide needs an inline 'Who should talk to a doctor' or 'Contraindications' section. Highest priority.

### ashwagandha-guide
- **Claim:** A 2012 double-blind RCT by Chandrasekhar et al. ... showed a 27.9% reduction in serum cortisol
  - **Critique:** The Chandrasekhar 2012 trial had only 64 participants, was funded by Ixoreal Biomed (KSM-66's manufacturer), and published in a journal with modest impact factor. Citing it as the cornerstone of 'strong evidence' without disclosing industry funding is a significant omission. Readers making a $40+/month purchasing decision deserve to know who paid for the study. The guide never once mentions funding sources for any cited trial — a pattern that consistently inflates confidence in industry-sponsored research.
- **Claim:** Multiple RCTs consistently show ashwagandha reduces serum cortisol by 20–30% over 8–12 weeks. This is one of the strongest effect sizes in herbal supplement research.
  - **Critique:** This claim appears twice in close proximity (once after the Chandrasekhar citation, once after Lopresti), indicating a copy-paste error that undermines editorial credibility. More critically, 'one of the strongest effect sizes in herbal supplement research' is an unanchored superlative — compared to what? No comparator is provided. The cortisol reductions are measured from baseline within the ashwagandha group, not always versus placebo-adjusted delta, which inflates apparent effect size due to regression to the mean.
- **Claim:** Ashwagandha has GABAergic and serotonergic activity, meaning it could potentiate the effects of SSRIs, benzodiazepines, or other CNS-active medications.
  - **Critique:** This paragraph is duplicated nearly verbatim — once in the FAQ and once just above it — with minor rewording. This is a critical section (drug interactions) being presented twice, which signals sloppy editing and may confuse readers about whether they missed something. One version should be removed.
- **Claim:** We don't have 5-year safety data from controlled studies... a 2020 safety review found ashwagandha well-tolerated in doses up to 1,250mg daily for 30 days.
  - **Critique:** The guide completely omits the FDA MedWatch reports and published case reports (2021–2023) of ashwagandha-associated hepatotoxicity, some requiring hospitalization. Several were linked to products containing high leaf content (higher withaferin A). This is not a rare-disease footnote — it's material safety information that any responsible guide published after 2021 must address. Omitting it while citing a 2020 safety review creates a false sense of established safety.

### beginner-longevity-supplement-stack
- **Claim:** REDUCE-IT showed a 25% reduction in cardiovascular events with high-dose EPA
  - **Critique:** REDUCE-IT used a mineral oil placebo that may have increased LDL-C and inflammation in the control group, artificially inflating the apparent benefit. Multiple independent analyses have questioned whether the benefit is real or an artifact of the flawed placebo. The guide presents this as clean evidence when it is one of the most contested trials in recent cardiology. A reader who Googles this will find the controversy immediately and lose trust in the guide's objectivity.
- **Claim:** people in the highest Omega-3 Index quartile lived an estimated 5 years longer — comparable to the mortality impact of smoking
  - **Critique:** This is an observational association from one study, not a causal estimate. Conflating an observational quartile comparison with the well-established causal harm of smoking is a classic epidemiological overreach. High omega-3 index could simply be a marker of generally healthy diet and lifestyle. The guide rightly criticizes 'running ahead of human evidence' elsewhere but then commits the same sin here with its most dramatic statistic.
- **Claim:** Magnesium glycinate: 300–400mg, evening — for general use
  - **Critique:** The guide recommends 300–400mg elemental magnesium but then lists magnesium glycinate in the practical stack. Glycinate is typically sold as 200–400mg of magnesium glycinate containing only ~50mg elemental magnesium per capsule. The distinction between elemental magnesium dose and compound dose is skipped entirely, virtually guaranteeing readers will under-dose or be confused when they read supplement labels. This is a concrete, actionable error.

### berberine-guide
- **Claim:** In the landmark Yin 2008 trial, berberine lowered HbA1c by 2.0% vs. metformin's 1.7% in newly diagnosed type 2 diabetics.
  - **Critique:** The guide treats this single 13-week, 116-patient Chinese trial as near-definitive evidence that berberine equals or beats metformin. Critical omissions: (1) the study was conducted in a population with very high baseline HbA1c (~9.5%), where regression to the mean inflates apparent treatment effects; (2) it was not blinded; (3) it was never replicated in a Western population with different dietary patterns and gut microbiomes; (4) metformin dosing in the trial (1500mg/day) was on the lower end of typical clinical dosing. Presenting this as 'comparable to metformin' without those caveats is misleading and will be challenged by any physician a reader consults.
- **Claim:** Berberine inhibits CYP3A4, CYP2D6, and P-glycoprotein — the same metabolic pathways that process many prescription drugs.
  - **Critique:** The guide names the enzymes but never gives readers a single concrete example of a dangerous interaction. CYP3A4 substrates include statins (simvastatin, atorvastatin), cyclosporine, warfarin, many SSRIs, and oral contraceptives. CYP2D6 substrates include tamoxifen, codeine, metoprolol. A reader on statins, antidepressants, or birth control — all extremely common — needs to know this explicitly, not just 'talk to your doctor.' The vagueness here is a genuine safety gap, not a nitpick.
- **Claim:** People with normal blood sugar who are just looking for a 'longevity boost' — berberine is unlikely to provide meaningful benefit and you're taking on side effect risk for minimal reward.
  - **Critique:** This is the right conclusion but the reasoning is too thin. The guide never addresses the large and growing segment of readers taking berberine for longevity purposes based on AMPK/mTOR podcasts (Huberman, Attia, etc.). These readers need to know: (1) no long-term human data exists on AMPK activation translating to lifespan extension; (2) chronic AMPK activation may blunt hypertrophic signaling (mTOR), potentially harming muscle protein synthesis in older or training adults — a real concern with no mention in the guide; (3) the 'exercise mimetic' framing should not imply it replaces exercise.

### best-magnesium-supplements
- **Claim:** Magnesium glycinate absorbs at closer to 80%
  - **Critique:** This 80% figure is stated without citation and is almost certainly inflated. The Firoz & Graber (2001) paper cited for oxide's 4% does not establish glycinate at 80%. Most peer-reviewed comparisons show glycinate/bisglycinate bioavailability in the 40-60% range relative to other forms, not an absolute 80%. Presenting an uncited 80% next to a cited 4% creates a false precision that will be challenged by any informed reader and could undermine the guide's credibility.
- **Claim:** The key study (Slutsky et al., 2010, Neuron) demonstrated improved learning, working memory, and both short- and long-term memory in animal models
  - **Critique:** The guide leads with an animal study to justify a premium-priced human supplement. While Liu et al. 2016 is mentioned for human data, the framing buries the critical limitation: the Slutsky study was in rats. A skeptical reader will notice this and question the entire threonate recommendation. The guide should foreground that human evidence is limited and the Liu et al. trial had only 44 participants — insufficient to make strong efficacy claims.
- **Claim:** Magnesium taurate is the form with the most emerging evidence for blood pressure and heart rhythm support
  - **Critique:** The cited Zhang et al. 2018 meta-analysis covers magnesium supplementation broadly, not taurate specifically. There is no citation provided establishing taurate as superior to other forms for cardiovascular outcomes. The blood pressure reduction (2-3 mmHg) is attributed to the mineral, not the taurate complex. The claim that taurate has 'the most emerging evidence' among forms is unsupported and potentially misleading.

### best-omega-3-supplements
- **Claim:** A 2010 study by Dyerberg et al. found TG-form omega-3s had approximately 70% better absorption than ethyl ester form
  - **Critique:** The Dyerberg 2010 study is a single, small, industry-cited trial and is far from settled science. Subsequent research (e.g., Neubronner et al., 2011, European Journal of Clinical Nutrition) showed EE form can perform comparably to rTG when taken with high-fat meals. The guide presents this as consensus when it's actually contested. A reader on a budget who buys a quality EE product and takes it with food is not necessarily leaving absorption on the table, and this framing may push them toward an unnecessary price premium.
- **Claim:** The VITAL trial and REDUCE-IT trial used 1,000–4,000mg of combined EPA + DHA daily
  - **Critique:** This conflates two very different trials with fundamentally different designs. VITAL used 840mg EPA+DHA (not 1,000–4,000mg) and found null results for primary cardiovascular endpoints. REDUCE-IT used 4g/day of pure EPA (icosapentaenoic acid, not combined EPA+DHA) and is mired in controversy over whether the mineral oil placebo inflated the results by raising LDL in the control group. Citing both in the same breath without these distinctions is misleading and could cause readers to believe standard fish oil dosing has stronger trial support than it does.
- **Claim:** The Dose-Response for Specific Outcomes section references '2:1 and EPA dose exceeds 1,000 mg' as a recommendation
  - **Critique:** This section appears to be broken — the rendered content includes raw component prop strings ('2:1 and EPA dose exceeds 1,000 mg.' recommendation='DHA-heavy fish oils won't replicate these results.'). This is a production rendering bug that makes the section unreadable and damages credibility with any informed reader who reaches it. This is a blocking editorial defect, not just a content gap.

### best-pre-workout-supplement-protocol

  - **Critique:** This single study (Pérez-Guisado & Jakeman) is routinely cited in marketing contexts but has methodological concerns: small N (41), single-sex male sample, and the 52.9% figure represents a relative improvement from a very low baseline. Presenting this as 'landmark' without acknowledging the mixed replication record is misleading and will be challenged by any informed reader.

  - **Critique:** The Wolfe citation here is mischaracterized. Wolfe's 2017 JISSN paper actually argued in partial defense of BCAAs under specific conditions. The more appropriate citation for the 'BCAAs are redundant if protein is adequate' argument is Wolfe (2017) read alongside Devries & Phillips (2015) or the Morton et al. meta-analysis. Getting the citation direction wrong is a credibility issue that informed readers will catch.
- **Claim:** The Protocol: Putting It All Together
  - **Critique:** This section header exists but appears to have no content rendered — the guide jumps straight to FAQs. This is either a rendering bug or a missing table/summary section. A reader who scrolled to find a concrete 'do this' protocol will find nothing and bounce. This is the most conversion-critical section of the page and it's empty.

### best-vitamin-d-supplements
- **Claim:** If you're taking 1,000 IU or less, you're probably fine without K2 (assuming a reasonable diet)
  - **Critique:** This threshold appears to be the author's heuristic with no cited evidence. The Knapen 2017 study cited pertains to K2's effect on arterial stiffness in postmenopausal women — it does not establish a 'safe' vitamin D dose below which K2 is unnecessary. Chronic vitamin D supplementation at even 1,000 IU can meaningfully increase calcium absorption in individuals with low K2 dietary intake (e.g., those not eating fermented foods or aged cheeses). The 'assuming a reasonable diet' qualifier is doing enormous lifting here and is undefined. This sends users with low K2 diets (most Americans) toward a potentially risky practice.
- **Claim:** 50–80 ng/mL: Some longevity researchers (including Dr. Rhonda Patrick and the Vitamin D Council) suggest targeting 40–60 ng/mL
  - **Critique:** The Vitamin D Council is a disbanded advocacy group, not a peer-reviewed body, and citing it alongside a podcaster as equivalent authority to the Endocrine Society is a credibility problem. More critically, the 2024 VITAL trial and subsequent re-analyses found no significant benefit for outcomes like cancer incidence or cardiovascular events at higher serum levels in a large RCT, and some observational data shows a U-shaped mortality curve above 60 ng/mL. Framing the 50–80 range as a legitimate 'optimal' target without noting that RCT evidence does not robustly support benefits of levels above 50 ng/mL is misleading to longevity-focused readers.
- **Claim:** Prescription vitamin D is often D2 (ergocalciferol) in a 50,000 IU weekly dose. Ask your provider about switching to daily D3
  - **Critique:** This is actionable medical advice to override a physician's prescription without adequate context. Weekly high-dose D2 is a clinically validated repletion protocol with decades of evidence and is sometimes chosen deliberately (compliance, cost, insurance coverage). The guide presents daily D3 as strictly superior without acknowledging that the pharmacokinetics of weekly high-dose D2 for repletion vs. daily D3 for maintenance are being conflated. A reader may stop a medically supervised repletion course prematurely.
- **Claim:** The Endocrine Society considers up to 10,000 IU/day the tolerable upper limit for adults
  - **Critique:** The Endocrine Society's 2011 guideline does reference 10,000 IU as the no-observed-adverse-effect level (NOAEL), but the Institute of Medicine (now National Academy of Medicine) sets the Tolerable Upper Intake Level (UL) at 4,000 IU/day for adults. These are different standards with different methodologies. Citing the higher figure without acknowledging the more conservative IOM UL — which most clinicians reference — gives readers a falsely permissive upper bound. This could directly encourage unsafe self-dosing.

### collagen-guide
- **Claim:** A 2005 study by Iwai et al. confirmed that Pro-Hyp and other collagen-specific peptides appear in the bloodstream within 1–2 hours of ingestion and persist for several hours.
  - **Critique:** The guide presents a single 2005 study as the foundational proof of the bioactive peptide mechanism without noting critical limitations: the Iwai study used small sample sizes and was conducted in Japanese subjects consuming collagen-rich food (not supplements). The guide never addresses whether the concentrations detected in blood are actually high enough to produce meaningful fibroblast stimulation in vivo — 'physiologically relevant' is asserted but not quantified or sourced. This is the central mechanistic claim of the entire guide and deserves more rigorous citation, including more recent pharmacokinetic work.

  - **Critique:** The guide presents a Caco-2 cell (in vitro) finding as evidence for human bioavailability without flagging the critical limitation: Caco-2 is a cancer cell line used as a proxy for intestinal absorption, not a human absorption study. Translating Caco-2 results to actual human bioavailability is scientifically contested. Telling readers marine is '1.5x better absorbed' based solely on this will mislead purchasing decisions. No human pharmacokinetic studies comparing marine vs. bovine are cited, because very few exist.
- **Claim:** No serious adverse events have been reported in clinical trials at standard doses up to 15g daily.
  - **Critique:** This is accurate for generally healthy adults in short-term trials but dangerously incomplete. The guide never mentions: (1) heavy metal contamination risk in marine collagen from polluted waters — a real and documented quality concern; (2) hypercalcemia risk in people with certain conditions taking bone broth-adjacent collagen sources; (3) no safety data exists for pregnant or breastfeeding women, yet the guide never flags this population. Omitting these gives false confidence.
- **Claim:** The evidence for improved skin elasticity and hydration is solid and replicated.
  - **Critique:** The 2019 de Miranda systematic review cited includes studies that are largely industry-funded (by Rousselot, Gelita, and similar collagen manufacturers). The guide never discloses industry funding conflicts across the cited literature, which is a significant credibility gap. A 2021 Cochrane-style critique of this literature noted that most positive collagen trials have high risk of bias due to industry sponsorship and small sample sizes. Calling the evidence 'solid' without this caveat is misleading.

### do-you-need-a-multivitamin
- **Claim:** folic acid (can't be converted by 40% of people)
  - **Critique:** This is a significant distortion of the MTHFR literature. The C677T polymorphism appears in ~10-15% homozygous and ~40-50% heterozygous forms, but heterozygotes have only mildly reduced conversion capacity and rarely show functional folate deficiency from standard folic acid doses. Presenting '40% of people can't convert it' as a flat fact is supplement-marketing shorthand that will mislead readers into unnecessary methylfolate purchases. Needs explicit nuance about homozygous vs. heterozygous and actual clinical impact.
- **Claim:** The JAMA Network Open meta-analysis (2024): no significant reduction in all-cause mortality. Zero.
  - **Critique:** Citing 'Zero' is rhetorically persuasive but analytically incomplete. All-cause mortality is a blunt endpoint — many nutrients supplemented have plausible mechanisms for disease-specific but not all-cause mortality benefits. The guide uses this finding to broadly dismiss multivitamins but then endorses targeted supplementation with D3, magnesium, and omega-3 — none of which have robust all-cause mortality RCT evidence either. The standard being applied asymmetrically weakens the argument.

### electrolytes-guide
- **Claim:** The PURE study found lowest risk in the range of 3,000–6,000mg of sodium per day — well above the 2,300mg upper limit
  - **Critique:** PURE has been heavily criticized for methodological issues: it used spot urine samples to estimate 24-hour sodium excretion (Kawasaki method), which has significant measurement error; it included populations with high baseline cardiovascular disease risk from low-income countries where low sodium may proxy for malnutrition; and it was observational with known confounders. The guide presents PURE's findings as near-definitive without mentioning these critiques, which is exactly what cherry-picked evidence looks like. Readers who Google 'PURE study criticism' will find substantial pushback from cardiologists.
- **Claim:** Drink to thirst rather than forcing a fixed volume — your body's thirst mechanism is more reliable than any formula
  - **Critique:** This advice is correct for sedentary adults but actively dangerous in specific populations: older adults (thirst sensation declines significantly with age), endurance athletes in heat (thirst lags behind actual fluid needs during high-intensity exercise), and people taking medications that blunt thirst (e.g., some antipsychotics, anticholinergics). The guide's contraindications section covers kidney/heart conditions but omits older adults and medicated individuals for this specific point.
- **Claim:** The DIY recipe: 1/4 teaspoon NoSalt (potassium chloride — roughly 650mg potassium)
  - **Critique:** The guide recommends NoSalt/NuSalt freely in the recipe but only buries a kidney disease warning in the contraindications section. Potassium chloride salt substitutes carry an FDA label warning precisely because consumers self-administer them without knowing their kidney function. More critically, the guide does not warn that the combination of potassium chloride and certain medications (ACE inhibitors, ARBs, NSAIDs, trimethoprim) can cause life-threatening hyperkalemia. This is a safety gap that exposes significant liability.

- **Claim:** Step 4 heading 'Ignoring Interactions and Redundancy' appears with no body content beneath it

Leaving this blank is the single most damaging structural flaw in the guide.

- **Claim:** Nutritional yeast and certain algae contain B12 analogues that can actually interfere with true B12 absorption.
  - **Needs:** Needs citation. The primary evidence is Watanabe et al. (2002) in the American Journal of Clinical Nutrition studying dried purple laver (nori) in vegans, which showed inactive corrinoids. The interference mechanism study is Dagnelie et al. (1991) in spirulina. Without citations, this reads as unsupported negative marketing against food sources.
- **Claim:** From a 1,000mcg tablet, you absorb roughly 12–22mcg total.
  - **Needs:** The passive diffusion rate of ~1–2% is well-established (Carmel, 2008 review in Annual Review of Medicine), but the combined absorption calculation should cite a specific pharmacokinetic study. The range given (12–22mcg) implies precision that isn't supported by a cited source, and it varies significantly by GI health status.
- **Claim:** A 2006 study in the British Journal of Clinical Pharmacology (Sharabi et al.) found sublingual B12 to be clinically effective for correcting deficiency in patients with gastrointestinal malabsorption.
  - **Needs:** The Sharabi study had 10 patients — it is far too small to be cited as establishing clinical effectiveness for a broad population recommendation. A larger study or systematic review of sublingual delivery (e.g., Delpre et al. 1999, or a 2018 meta-analysis by Bensky et al.) should be cited or the sample size caveat disclosed.
- **Claim:** Up to 86% of vegans and up to 50% of long-term vegetarians had B12 deficiency or insufficiency when not supplementing.
  - **Needs:** The Pawlak 2013 meta-analysis is the correct cite, but the guide should note that the 86% figure pools studies with varying deficiency definitions (some using serum B12 <200, others <300 pg/mL) and includes populations from developing countries with different dietary patterns. The figure may overstate risk for well-nourished Western vegans eating fortified foods. A more granular breakdown by supplementation status and geography would serve readers better.

### what-to-look-for-in-a-probiotic
- **Claim:** Conventional Lactobacillus probiotics can worsen SIBO symptoms by adding to the bacterial load in the wrong part of the gut
  - **Needs:** A specific citation is required — e.g., Rao et al. (2018) in Clinical and Translational Gastroenterology ('Brain Fogginess, Gas and Bloating: A Link Between SIBO, Poor Carb Digestion and Brain Fog') is frequently cited for this, but it was a small retrospective study. The guide should acknowledge the evidence is low-quality and contested rather than presenting it as consensus.
- **Claim:** Taking probiotics with a meal (especially one containing some fat) is optimal for conventional capsules
  - **Needs:** Specifically cite Tompkins et al. (2011) in Beneficial Microbes, which is the primary source for this recommendation. Without citation, this reads as bro-science despite being reasonably well-supported.
- **Claim:** Most probiotic benefits appear between day 10 and day 21
  - **Needs:** This specific timeframe needs a citation or range source. The onset window varies dramatically by strain and condition — LGG for AAD shows effects within the antibiotic course (days); IBS trials typically run 4–8 weeks before primary endpoints. The 10–21 day claim lacks sourcing and may create false expectations.
- **Claim:** Fiber, hydration, and magnesium oxide/citrate all outperform probiotics for chronic constipation
  - **Needs:** The comparative claim needs support. Magnesium for constipation has reasonable evidence (mainly osmotic mechanism), but a direct head-to-head with probiotics in chronic constipation is not well-established. Citing Ford et al. (2014) Gut or similar systematic reviews on constipation management hierarchy would validate this.
- **Claim:** BB-12 (B. animalis subsp. lactis): Evidence for bowel regularity and some immune markers; results in healthy adults more mixed than in infants
  - **Needs:** The guide lists BB-12 as a top-tier strain in the strains section but then downgrades it in the evidence hierarchy section with no specific trials cited in either direction. A specific RCT (e.g., Eskesen et al. 2015 in British Journal of Nutrition for bowel regularity) and an explanation of where the adult evidence falls short would resolve the contradiction.

### zinc-guide
- **Claim:** Zinc supplementation raises testosterone in people who are zinc deficient
  - **Needs:** The Prasad 1996 study is foundational but used a specific experimental depletion design (not naturalistic deficiency) and measured effects in young men and elderly men — not the broad adult male population. A systematic review of zinc and testosterone across naturalistic deficiency populations would strengthen this claim, or an honest acknowledgment that no such review exists and the translation from experimental depletion to real-world supplementation is uncertain.
- **Claim:** Zinc bisglycinate is gentle on the stomach / excellent choice for nausea
  - **Needs:** No citation is provided for bisglycinate's GI tolerability advantage. A head-to-head tolerability RCT or a citation to clinical evidence comparing GI adverse events across zinc forms is needed.
- **Claim:** Zinc + vitamin D — zinc is a cofactor in vitamin D receptor signaling; the two work synergistically for immune function
  - **Needs:** The 'work synergistically' claim needs a citation. Zinc's role in VDR signaling has mechanistic plausibility but clinical evidence for immune synergy from co-supplementation in humans is limited. This claim as stated implies clinical outcomes data that doesn't clearly exist.
- **Claim:** A 2014 meta-analysis in Dermatology Research and Practice found that oral zinc supplementation reduced inflammatory acne lesion counts
  - **Needs:** The guide does not provide authors or full citation for this meta-analysis, making it impossible to verify. Author name and sample size of included trials should be provided. A more recent 2020 systematic review in the Journal of Dermatological Treatment (Yee et al.) covers this topic more comprehensively and should replace or supplement this reference.
- **Claim:** Zinc oxide — absorption is roughly 50% lower than picolinate or bisglycinate
  - **Needs:** A specific citation is absent here. The closest supporting study would be Wegmüller et al. (2014, Journal of Nutrition) or the Barrie 1987 study — but neither is cited in this context. The guide needs a direct source for the 50% figure.
