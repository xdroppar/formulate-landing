## Agent A: Blocking Safety/Contraindication Sections (29)

These are YMYL liability issues. Each guide needs an inline 'Who should talk to a doctor' or 'Contraindications' section. Highest priority.

### ashwagandha-for-testosterone
- **Claim:** A 2022 systematic review by Smith et al. pooled five RCTs and concluded ashwagandha had a 'statistically significant but clinically modest' effect on testosterone.
  - **Critique:** No 'Smith et al. 2022' systematic review on ashwagandha and testosterone is verifiable in the literature. The most cited relevant meta-analysis is Durg et al. (2019) in Phytomedicine, and a 2023 review by Tazumi et al. The guide should cite a real, verifiable paper — this either is a hallucinated citation or a pseudonymized source that readers cannot look up. This is a serious credibility problem for a guide claiming to show 'what the evidence actually shows.'
- **Claim:** Rare hepatotoxicity — a small number of case reports describe liver injury, though causality is debated and confounders are common.
  - **Critique:** Since 2021, regulatory agencies including the MHRA (UK) and Health Canada have issued formal warnings about ashwagandha-associated hepatotoxicity. Describing this as merely 'case reports with debated causality' understates the current regulatory posture. Readers on hepatotoxic medications (statins, acetaminophen users, alcohol drinkers) are particularly underserved by this framing.

### berberine-vs-metformin
- **Claim:** Dihydroberberine...shows roughly 5x higher absorption in animal models, though human data is limited.
  - **Critique:** The guide mentions dihydroberberine absorbs 5x better but then drops the thread. A reader who has already seen dihydroberberine marketed aggressively (e.g., Berberine Phytosome, GlucoVantage) will immediately Google it after reading this. The guide doesn't tell them whether the clinical trials showing HbA1c benefit used standard HCl form, meaning dihydroberberine's 'equivalence' to those outcomes is entirely unproven. This omission is a critical conversion and credibility gap.
- **Claim:** Berberine crosses the placenta and has shown teratogenic effects in animal studies. It is contraindicated during pregnancy.
  - **Critique:** No citation is provided for the teratogenicity claim. For a contraindication this serious, readers deserve a specific reference. The underlying animal data (e.g., studies showing fetal toxicity in rats) should be cited so the claim isn't perceived as boilerplate legal hedging. Absence of a citation here is a credibility problem for a guide that otherwise cites sources.

### best-collagen-for-joints
- **Claim:** UC-II has the strongest joint-specific evidence — two randomized controlled trials showed it outperformed the longstanding joint supplement standard of glucosamine plus chondroitin
  - **Critique:** Both cited trials (Crowley 2009, n=52; Lugo 2016, n=191) were funded by InterHealth Nutraceuticals, the patent holder. The guide acknowledges this but undersells the problem: industry-funded trials on proprietary ingredients systematically show effect sizes 30-40% larger than independent replications (as documented in Lundh et al., Cochrane 2017). The guide frames UC-II as having 'more compelling evidence' than glucosamine+chondroitin, but the GAIT trial (Clegg et al. 2006, NEJM, n=1,583, independent NIH funding) showed glucosamine+chondroitin failed to beat placebo for most OA subgroups — meaning UC-II is beating a weak comparator. This context is entirely missing and would materially change how a reader weighs the evidence.

### best-multivitamin-over-50
- **Claim:** Calcium supplements (without co-administered vitamin D) were associated with a modest increase in cardiovascular events... The resolution... is twofold: pair calcium with vitamin D3 and vitamin K2
  - **Critique:** This framing overstates resolution of a genuinely unresolved debate. Subsequent re-analyses (Bolland et al., 2015 BMJ; Reid et al., 2015) maintained the cardiovascular signal even when D3 was included. The USPSTF (2018) explicitly recommended against calcium + D3 supplementation for primary fracture prevention in noninstitutionalized postmenopausal women. The guide presents K2 as a definitive fix based on Knapen et al. (2015), a single RCT in 244 women — that's not a resolved scientific consensus. Readers taking this at face value may over-supplement calcium under false reassurance.
- **Claim:** Many older adults need 4,000–5,000 IU to reach optimal serum levels of 40–60 ng/mL
  - **Critique:** The '40–60 ng/mL is optimal' claim is contested. The IOM set 20 ng/mL as sufficient, and the Endocrine Society's 'at risk' threshold of 30 ng/mL differs from the 40 ng/mL floor this guide presents as fact. Recommending 4,000–5,000 IU without qualification is problematic — at those doses, especially in older adults with impaired vitamin D metabolism, toxicity risk increases. There is no RCT showing mortality or fracture benefit at 40–60 ng/mL vs. 30 ng/mL in the general older adult population. The VITAL trial (Manson et al., 2019, NEJM) used 2,000 IU and found no fracture benefit — context the guide omits entirely.
- **Claim:** The best multivitamin for adults over 50 isn't just a standard formula with 'Silver' slapped on the label
  - **Critique:** The guide's top pick (Thorne Advanced Nutrients 2/Day) retails at ~$40–55/month. The guide never addresses price as a variable, never includes a budget option, and never acknowledges that Thorne is a premium brand unavailable in most physical retail stores. For the significant portion of the 50+ population on fixed incomes or Medicare, this pick is functionally inaccessible with no fallback guidance.

### creatine-for-endurance-athletes
- **Claim:** The typical weight gain from creatine saturation is 1–2 lbs (0.5–1 kg)... For virtually all endurance athletes: no [it won't hurt performance].
  - **Critique:** This blanket dismissal ignores meaningful edge cases beyond 'elite mountain climbers.' For weight-class-sensitive events (lightweight rowing, cycling hill climbs where w/kg is paramount), or athletes who are already water-retaining due to hormonal factors, even 0.7kg matters. The guide also never quantifies the magnitude of performance benefits in watts or pace units, so readers cannot actually do the cost-benefit math themselves. Saying benefits 'far outweigh' the cost without numbers is marketing language, not evidence.
- **Claim:** Robinson et al. (1999) demonstrated that creatine supplementation increased post-exercise glycogen storage by roughly 9%
  - **Critique:** This is a single 25-year-old study with a small sample (n=9), used to support one of the guide's primary mechanisms for endurance athletes. The guide presents this as established fact in the summary section and the Bottom Line without acknowledging the replication problem. No subsequent studies confirming this glycogen finding are cited, and at least one later meta-analysis (Lanhers et al., 2017) did not elevate this as a robust finding. This is the guide's most important mechanistic claim for endurance-specific benefit and it rests on the weakest evidence presented.

### magnesium-for-anxiety
- **Claim:** Glycinate is the form with the strongest rationale because the glycine component itself acts as an inhibitory neurotransmitter, giving you a two-for-one neurochemical benefit.
  - **Critique:** The guide never quantifies how much glycine is actually delivered per dose. A 400 mg elemental magnesium dose from glycinate yields roughly 2,000–2,400 mg of magnesium glycinate salt, which contains approximately 1.5–1.8 g of glycine. The guide cites '3+ grams' as the threshold for independent anxiolytic/sleep effects from glycine, meaning a standard glycinate dose delivers only 50–60% of the evidenced glycine threshold. The 'two-for-one' framing is therefore misleading — you're getting sub-threshold glycine unless you're stacking. This is a blocking issue because it's central to the guide's unique thesis.
- **Claim:** Magnesium enhances the binding efficiency of GABA at GABA-A receptors, the same receptors targeted by benzodiazepines
  - **Critique:** This claim is stated without citation. The well-established role of magnesium is as an NMDA channel blocker — that evidence is solid. The GABA-A 'binding efficiency' enhancement claim is far less established in the human literature and appears to derive primarily from in-vitro or rodent electrophysiology. Presenting it as a 'well-characterized brain mechanism' equal in status to the NMDA block overstates the evidence and could mislead readers about potency relative to benzodiazepines.
- **Claim:** Boyle et al. (2017)... pooled data from 18 studies... showed a small-to-moderate effect size favoring magnesium over placebo
  - **Critique:** The guide omits a critical flaw acknowledged in the Boyle review itself: the majority of included studies were not randomized controlled trials, and the review authors explicitly noted the overall evidence quality was 'poor' due to lack of blinding, heterogeneous outcome measures, and high risk of bias. Describing a 'small-to-moderate effect size' without noting the low evidence quality creates false confidence. A reader who looks this up will immediately find these caveats and distrust the guide.
- **Claim:** The tolerable upper intake level for supplemental magnesium is 350 mg/day of elemental magnesium
  - **Critique:** The guide's Bottom Line protocol recommends 300–400 mg supplemental magnesium, yet earlier in the guide it states the UL is 350 mg/day. The protocol therefore recommends exceeding the UL without acknowledging this contradiction. This is internally inconsistent and medically irresponsible for a consumer-facing guide. Either justify exceeding the UL with evidence, or reconcile the protocol with the stated UL.

### nac-and-alcohol
- **Claim:** Chung et al. (2001) demonstrated in an animal model that NAC pre-treatment significantly attenuated alcohol-induced liver lipid peroxidation and maintained glutathione levels, while post-treatment showed no protective benefit.
  - **Critique:** The guide cites Chung et al. (2001) as the linchpin citation for the pre-vs-post timing claim, but provides no DOI, journal, or enough detail for a reader or fact-checker to verify it. 'Animal model' is doing a lot of work — species, dose, and ethanol equivalence matter enormously for translational validity. The guide elsewhere rightly caveat-labels animal data but doesn't apply that label consistently here, making the timing rule sound more settled than it is.
- **Claim:** Several animal studies suggest that NAC taken after alcohol exposure — when acetaldehyde is already circulating — may increase rather than decrease oxidative damage.
  - **Critique:** This is the guide's most consequential behavioral claim — 'don't take NAC the morning after' — yet it rests on unnamed 'several animal studies' with no citations. The pro-oxidant Fenton mechanism is real biochemistry, but the dose ranges in rodent studies rarely map cleanly to human OTC supplementation doses (600–1200 mg). Without citations or dose context, a skeptical reader will (correctly) Google this and find contradictory advice, causing pogo-stick behavior.
- **Claim:** If you need pain relief after drinking, ibuprofen or aspirin are generally preferred over acetaminophen, though they carry their own GI risks with alcohol.
  - **Critique:** This is dangerously incomplete. NSAIDs (ibuprofen, aspirin, naproxen) with alcohol meaningfully increase bleeding risk and risk of gastric ulceration/hemorrhage — not a 'minor GI risk' footnote. For readers with a history of GI bleeds, ulcers, or who are on blood thinners (a segment explicitly flagged elsewhere in the guide), this framing could cause serious harm. The guide should either specify safer alternatives (e.g., lower-dose acetaminophen with food if truly not drinking heavily) or direct readers to a pharmacist for individualized guidance.

### probiotic-strains-for-ibs

VSL#3 (now Visbiome) has RCT data specifically for IBS-D and IBS-C. Bifidobacterium lactis BB-12 is mentioned briefly for IBS-C but then dismissed. This conflates evidence from non-IBS diarrhea contexts with IBS-D specifically. The guide should cite IBS-specific RCTs for S. boulardii (e.g., Choi et al. 2011, or Guslandi et al. 2000) rather than a broad diarrhea meta-analysis. A skeptical reader will notice that 'multiple GI conditions' could mean traveler's diarrhea or antibiotic-associated diarrhea — not IBS.

  - **Critique:** The guide never addresses the critical generalizability problem: this trial enrolled only women. The guide later claims 'subtype-agnostic evidence' and recommends B. infantis 35624 broadly, including to presumably male readers, without acknowledging this limitation. This is a material omission that a gastroenterologist would immediately flag, and male IBS sufferers (roughly 30-40% of IBS cases) deserve to know the primary RCT excluded them.

### vitamin-b12-methylcobalamin-vs-cyanocobalamin
- **Claim:** A randomized crossover trial by Okuda et al. (1973) found that methylcobalamin produced higher serum B12 retention at 48 hours compared to cyanocobalamin
  - **Critique:** Citing a 1973 trial as primary evidence for bioavailability superiority is a significant credibility problem. The guide then cites Paul & Brady (2017) but doesn't name the journal, sample size, or study design. Readers and search evaluators will notice the best bioavailability evidence is 50+ years old. More critically, multiple systematic reviews (including Vidal-Alaball et al., Cochrane 2005) found no clinically meaningful difference in deficiency correction between forms at equivalent doses — this contradictory evidence is entirely absent.
- **Claim:** methylcobalamin crosses the blood-brain barrier more readily (Izumi & Kaji, 2007)
  - **Critique:** This claim does significant lifting in the 'Who Should Choose Methylcobalamin' section but the citation appears to reference a Japanese-language clinical report, not a controlled trial. No mechanism, effect size, or study population is given. If the blood-brain barrier claim is unsubstantiated, the neurological symptom recommendation loses its strongest justification. The guide should either provide the actual study details or downgrade the claim to 'theorized' or 'preliminary.'
- **Claim:** Converting cyanocobalamin to methylcobalamin requires adequate methylation capacity — the very thing that's compromised [in MTHFR carriers]
  - **Critique:** This is a common supplement-marketing oversimplification. MTHFR variants affect the folate cycle (5,10-methyleneTHF → 5-MTHF), not directly the B12 conversion enzymes. Cyanocobalamin is converted to methylcobalamin via methionine synthase reductase and other enzymes, not primarily through the MTHFR-dependent pathway. The guide conflates two distinct pathways, creating a false mechanistic chain. While methylcobalamin may still be preferable for MTHFR carriers, the stated reason is biochemically imprecise and would be challenged by any biochemistry-literate reader.

### zinc-for-immune-support
- **Claim:** Zinc lozenges providing 80–92 mg/day of elemental zinc shortened cold duration by approximately 33% when started within 24 hours of symptom onset
  - **Critique:** The guide cites only Hemilä (2017) throughout, presenting it as near-definitive. However, Hemilä is the same researcher across most of the primary zinc lozenge trials and meta-analyses, raising potential investigator bias. The 2021 Cochrane review on zinc for the common cold (Science et al.) found more mixed results across a broader trial set and noted significant heterogeneity. Citing only one researcher's meta-analysis without acknowledging conflicting analyses (e.g., the broader Cochrane evidence base) misrepresents the consensus strength. A reader who finds the Cochrane review will feel misled.
- **Claim:** 75–92 mg/day of zinc is well above the tolerable upper intake level of 40 mg/day for ongoing use. This dose is intended for acute use over 3–5 days only.
  - **Critique:** The guide normalizes exceeding the UL more than 2x daily for up to a week without adequately explaining what 'tolerable upper intake level' means (it's the threshold above which adverse effects become likely with chronic use, set by the Institute of Medicine/National Academies). The guide does not address what happens if someone has a prolonged cold that runs 7–10 days — should they stop the protocol? Switch forms? This is a real clinical scenario the guide leaves hanging, which a reader will immediately Google.
- **Claim:** Glycine... bind free zinc ions and prevent them from reaching tissue
  - **Critique:** Glycine is listed alongside citric acid, tartaric acid, and mannitol as a chelating additive to avoid. However, zinc bisglycinate (zinc bound to glycine) is actually recommended in the chronic supplementation section of the same guide. This is internally contradictory and will confuse readers. The guide needs to distinguish between glycine as a lozenge additive that chelates ionized zinc in saliva versus bisglycinate as an oral absorption form for systemic supplementation.
