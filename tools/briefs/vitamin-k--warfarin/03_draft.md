# Stage 3 — Draft

Pair: **Vitamin K** + **Warfarin**

## Summary

Vitamin K directly antagonizes warfarin. Consistency of intake, not avoidance, is the right clinical approach.

## Details

### Mechanism

Warfarin works by blocking vitamin K epoxide reductase, preventing the regeneration of reduced vitamin K that's needed to activate clotting factors II, VII, IX, and X [strong, S1]. Adding exogenous vitamin K — whether from food or supplements — provides the substrate warfarin is trying to keep unavailable [strong, S1]. This is a textbook direct antagonism: more vitamin K, less warfarin effect, lower INR, higher clotting risk. Less vitamin K, more warfarin effect, higher INR, higher bleeding risk.

### Evidence quality

This is one of the best-characterized drug-nutrient interactions in medicine. A 2016 systematic review (S1) concluded the mechanism is real but that the *clinical* implication is often misstated [moderate, S1]. A key randomized trial (Sconce et al, N=70 warfarin patients with unstable INR control) showed that **low-dose daily vitamin K supplementation** (150 micrograms) actually *stabilized* INR in previously unstable patients [moderate, S2]. Prospective dietary tracking shows an inverse correlation between vitamin K intake variability and INR variability (r = -0.600, p<0.01) [moderate, S3]. The common thread: **consistency of intake is what matters, not absolute level**.

### Clinical relevance

The old advice — "avoid leafy greens on warfarin" — is out of date and, in some patients, actively harmful. Wildly inconsistent vitamin K intake (lots of kale one week, none the next) produces worse INR control than either consistent high intake or consistent low intake. For patients with chronic erratic INR on stable warfarin doses, a fixed low-dose vitamin K supplement is a legitimate clinical strategy [moderate, S2].

### Dose considerations

- **Dietary**: a single cup of cooked spinach contains ~900 micrograms of vitamin K1 — enough to measurably shift INR if added acutely to a low-intake baseline.
- **Supplemental**: standard K1 supplements (50-500 microgram) and K2 (MK-4, MK-7) products both contribute to the warfarin interaction.
- **Stabilization dose**: 150 micrograms/day has RCT support for INR stabilization in unstable patients [moderate, S2].

### Populations to watch

- **Newly-initiated warfarin patients**: do not dramatically change diet when starting. Inform the anticoagulation clinic of typical intake.
- **Patients with chronically unstable INR**: may benefit from consistent low-dose K supplementation rather than restriction.
- **Patients adding vitamin K2 (MK-7) for bone/cardiovascular reasons**: this does interact. Tell your prescriber.
- **Patients on DOACs (apixaban, rivaroxaban, dabigatran, edoxaban)**: NO vitamin K interaction. DOACs do not depend on vitamin K antagonism. This is important; many patients confuse the two classes.

### What we don't know

The quantitative relationship between dose change and INR shift is patient-specific; no universal formula applies. The specific comparability of K1 vs K2 (MK-4 vs MK-7) for warfarin interaction strength is not fully characterized.

## Recommendation

If you take warfarin, do not change your vitamin K intake — dietary or supplemental — without telling your anticoagulation clinic. Aim for consistent daily intake rather than restriction. If you want to start a vitamin K2 supplement (for bone or cardiovascular reasons), ask your prescriber to plan a warfarin re-titration and INR check 1-2 weeks after starting. If your INR is chronically unstable, ask whether a small consistent K supplement might help rather than hurt. If you are on a DOAC rather than warfarin, this interaction does not apply.

## Timing advice

Timing within the day does not materially help. The interaction is on the vitamin K cycle at the liver, not on absorption competition. Consistency day-to-day matters more than time-of-day.

## Severity: warning

Justification: The original INTERACTIONS entry was DANGER, but contemporary clinical practice treats this as WARNING — "meaningful clinical impact, mitigatable with monitoring." The mechanism is real and strong, but the clinical advice is "stay consistent" not "avoid." DANGER implies avoid-at-all-costs which is outdated and in some cases counterproductive. WARNING correctly signals: tell your prescriber, monitor INR, aim for consistency.
