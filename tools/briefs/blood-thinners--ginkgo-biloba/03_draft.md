# Stage 3 — Draft

Pair: **Blood thinners** + **Ginkgo biloba**

## Summary

Ginkgo may modestly raise bleeding risk with warfarin, aspirin, or clopidogrel. Evidence is mixed; precaution applies.

## Details

### Mechanism

Ginkgo biloba extracts contain ginkgolide B, a platelet-activating factor (PAF) antagonist that can inhibit platelet aggregation in vitro [moderate, S4]. The biochemical effect is real and well-characterized. The clinical question is whether it translates to meaningful bleeding risk when combined with warfarin, aspirin, or clopidogrel — and on that question, the evidence is genuinely split.

### Evidence quality

The largest observational study (Stoddard 2015, Veterans Administration, N=807,399 warfarin patients with a 1-year follow-up) found a hazard ratio of 1.38 (95% CI 1.20–1.58, p<0.001) for bleeding adverse events when ginkgo was co-administered with warfarin [moderate, S1]. That is a real population-level signal.

However, a more recent observational analysis (Mai 2025, N=2,647 prescriptions, Vietnam) did NOT find a significant warfarin-specific bleeding signal, though it DID find modest signals for ginkgo + aspirin (bleeding OR 1.12, 95% CI 1.07–1.18, p<0.001) and ginkgo + clopidogrel (bleeding OR 1.10, 95% CI 1.05–1.15, p<0.001) [moderate, S2]. The warfarin-specific divergence between S1 and S2 is unresolved and may reflect population, product, or management-intensity differences [weak, S2].

Controlled trials have been largely reassuring. Gardner 2007 randomized 55 older adults (mean age 69, cardiovascular risk) to EGb 761 300 mg/day or placebo alongside aspirin 325 mg/day for 4 weeks; no clinically or statistically significant differences appeared on any platelet aggregation or PFA-100 endpoint, and no bleeding events were reported [moderate, S3]. The most comprehensive systematic review (Bone 2008) concluded that controlled studies consistently show no significant impact on hemostasis and that the quality of published case reports implicating ginkgo in bleeding is low [moderate, S4].

### Clinical relevance

Interpreted honestly: the mechanism is real but the clinical magnitude appears modest. A 38% relative increase in bleeding (S1) is meaningful in a warfarin-anticoagulated population where baseline bleeding risk is already elevated, but not catastrophic. The effect has not been reliably reproduced in controlled settings. This pattern — real population signal, negative RCTs — often reflects confounding-by-indication in observational data (patients who take ginkgo may differ from those who don't in ways that correlate with bleeding risk).

### Dose considerations

Standardized extracts (EGb 761, 300 mg/day in S3) have been tested without detectable platelet effect over 4 weeks [moderate, S3]. The S1 and S2 observational studies did not stratify by ginkgo dose or extract type, so dose-response cannot be derived. Lower-quality, non-standardized ginkgo products may carry different (possibly higher) interaction risk — but this is inference, not data.

### Populations to watch

- **Patients on warfarin**: largest observational signal (S1). Highest concern given therapeutic INR management.
- **Patients on aspirin**: S2 shows modest signal; S3 RCT is reassuring at EGb 761 300 mg/day + ASA 325 mg/day for 4 weeks.
- **Patients on clopidogrel**: S2 shows modest signal; no direct RCT.
- **Patients on DOACs (apixaban, rivaroxaban, dabigatran, edoxaban)**: not directly studied. Class extrapolation uncertain.
- **Patients scheduled for surgery or neuraxial anesthesia**: the precautionary concern is highest here; most pre-op guidance recommends stopping ginkgo 5–7 days before surgery.

### What we don't know

- No adequately powered RCT of ginkgo + warfarin with a clinical bleeding endpoint exists [weak, S4].
- Dose-response curve for any anticoagulant pair is not characterized.
- Whether standardized EGb 761 vs other extracts differ in risk is plausible but not proven.
- DOACs have not been directly studied.

## Recommendation

Do not self-start ginkgo if you are on warfarin, aspirin, clopidogrel, or any anticoagulant/antiplatelet — consult the clinician who manages your blood thinner first. If you are already taking both, do not stop abruptly; ask your prescriber whether to taper the ginkgo and whether to retest INR or clinical bleeding indicators. Watch for unusual bruising, prolonged bleeding from minor cuts, gum bleeding, nosebleeds, or dark stools. Stop ginkgo 5–7 days before any scheduled surgery or neuraxial procedure. For anyone on a DOAC, the same precaution applies — direct evidence is lacking, but the mechanism is class-general.

## Timing advice

Timing-based separation does not reliably mitigate this interaction. Ginkgolide B's PAF-antagonist activity is systemic once absorbed. Monitoring (INR for warfarin, clinical symptoms for antiplatelets) is the relevant mitigation.

## Severity: warning

Justification: The original INTERACTIONS entry was DANGER, but the actual evidence base is inconsistent enough that DANGER overstates the risk. WARNING fits: meaningful clinical impact (population-level bleeding signal in S1), mitigatable with prescriber involvement and clinical monitoring (INR for warfarin patients, symptom watching for antiplatelet patients), and the RCT evidence argues against catastrophic risk at typical doses. CAUTION would understate the genuine signal in a large observational cohort; DANGER overstates given negative RCTs and mixed observational data. WARNING is the honest call.
