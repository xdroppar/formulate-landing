# Stage 3 — Draft

Pair: **Dong quai** + **Warfarin**

## Summary

Dong quai contains natural coumarins and has potentiated warfarin in a published case, raising bleeding risk.

## Details

### Mechanism

Dong quai (*Angelica sinensis*) is a traditional Chinese herb whose root contains natural coumarin derivatives — ferulic acid, osthole, and related compounds [moderate, S2]. These compounds have documented antithrombotic and platelet-inhibitory activity in vitro [moderate, S2]. When combined with warfarin, the effect appears to be additive on the anticoagulation endpoint without altering warfarin's pharmacokinetics. Animal steady-state studies show dong quai significantly increases prothrombin time WITHOUT changing warfarin blood concentration, suggesting the interaction is pharmacodynamic, not pharmacokinetic [moderate, S2]. Because warfarin itself is structurally a coumarin, adding plant coumarins is mechanistically intuitive even without definitive human trial data.

### Evidence quality

The primary human evidence is a **single case report** [weak, S1]. Page & Lawrence, 1999, described a 46-year-old woman with atrial fibrillation who had been stable on warfarin. She self-initiated dong quai at 565 mg once to twice daily. Over 4 weeks her INR rose to 4.05 and then 4.90 — more than double her therapeutic baseline — without bleeding events. She stopped dong quai; INR returned to acceptable levels within a month. No other diet, medication, or health changes were identified. The washout pattern strengthens attribution, but a single case cannot establish population-level effect size.

Beyond that case, two systematic reviews (Warfarin Interactions with Medicinal Herbs 2014; Chinese Herbal-Warfarin Review 2015) list dong quai among herbs with documented warfarin potentiation, drawing primarily on the 1999 case + mechanism data [moderate, S2] [moderate, S3].

### Clinical relevance

One case report is thin evidence, but three factors keep this clinically relevant: (1) the mechanism is intuitive — adding coumarins to warfarin; (2) animal PD data support the direction of effect; (3) downside (bleeding on anticoagulation) is serious enough that precaution is warranted even with weak evidence. The interaction is not "do not take under any circumstance" but "do not add to a stable warfarin regimen without your prescriber knowing."

### Dose considerations

- **Case-report dose**: 565 mg 1-2x/day produced the observed effect.
- **Lower doses** have not been tested.
- **Hyperforin analogue**: unlike SJW, dong quai does not have a single well-characterized marker compound; commercial extracts vary widely in coumarin content, so "low dose" is hard to define reliably.

### Populations to watch

- **Warfarin patients, especially those using dong quai for perimenopausal symptoms** (dong quai's main consumer-marketing use)
- **Any other anticoagulant or antiplatelet patient**: aspirin, clopidogrel, DOACs — same mechanism applies.
- **Pre-surgical patients**: stop dong quai 1-2 weeks before elective surgery.

### What we don't know

No RCT has been done. The quantitative INR shift per dose of dong quai is unknown. Whether specific preparations (water extract vs ethanol tincture vs standardized capsule) differ in risk is unclear.

## Recommendation

If you take warfarin, do not start dong quai without telling your prescriber. If you already take both, don't abruptly stop — ask for an INR check and a plan to discontinue the dong quai with monitoring. Watch for unusual bruising, gum bleeding, nosebleeds, or dark stools. For perimenopausal symptoms (the most common reason people take dong quai), discuss alternatives with your prescriber — many options do not interact with warfarin.

## Timing advice

Spacing doses does not meaningfully help; the interaction is pharmacodynamic and operates on the anticoagulation endpoint, not on absorption.

## Severity: warning

Justification: DANGER would require either a larger case series, an RCT, or documented non-reversible harm. The single 1999 case had no bleeding events and INR normalized on discontinuation. WARNING fits: real mechanism, real case evidence, reversible on stopping, prescriber-oversight mitigates.
