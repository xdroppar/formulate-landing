# Stage 3 — Draft

Pair: **Cyclosporine** + **St John's wort**

## Summary

St John's wort substantially lowers cyclosporine levels and has caused documented transplant rejection. Do not combine.

## Details

### Mechanism

Hyperforin, the primary CYP-active constituent of St John's wort (*Hypericum perforatum*), activates the pregnane X receptor (PXR) and strongly induces cytochrome P450 3A4 and P-glycoprotein [strong, S4]. Cyclosporine is a narrow-therapeutic-index immunosuppressant metabolized by CYP3A4 and effluxed by P-gp [strong, S4]. Inducing both pathways accelerates cyclosporine clearance and reduces circulating drug levels. Because cyclosporine's therapeutic window is narrow, even a modest induction produces clinically meaningful under-exposure — and under-exposure after a transplant means rejection.

### Evidence quality

This is among the best-documented dangerous SJW interactions in the pharmacovigilance literature. Published case reports span multiple organ types: two heart transplant recipients developed acute rejection after self-starting SJW, with cyclosporine levels dropping substantially from therapeutic baseline [moderate, S1]. A case series (Mai-Bonde 2001) reported 4 additional transplant recipients — 2 heart, 1 kidney, 1 pancreas — whose cyclosporine levels fell 25-62% within 3-4 weeks of starting SJW, with graft rejection in all four [moderate, S2]. A comprehensive 2007 drug-interaction review (Borrelli & Izzo) catalogs these cases and explicitly names cyclosporine, tacrolimus, and sirolimus as immunosuppressants at risk [moderate, S3]. The 2020 Nicolussi review characterizes the CYP3A4/P-gp induction mechanism in detail [strong, S4].

### Clinical relevance

Transplant rejection is the defining non-reversible harm that makes this pair DANGER. In the documented cases, cyclosporine levels returned to therapeutic range after SJW was stopped, and rejection was treated — but graft function can be permanently reduced by a rejection episode, and not every rejection is successfully treated. This is not a theoretical risk; real patients have lost graft function from this combination [moderate, S1] [moderate, S2].

### Dose considerations

- **High-hyperforin SJW products** (>1 mg hyperforin/day) have caused the interaction at standard 600-900 mg/day doses.
- **Low-hyperforin products**: Nicolussi 2020 notes reduced PK interactions with low-hyperforin extracts, but given the non-reversible consequence, transplant physicians universally advise against any SJW use regardless of product labeling [moderate, S4].
- **Any therapeutic cyclosporine dose** is at risk.

### Populations to watch

- **All organ transplant recipients on cyclosporine, tacrolimus, or sirolimus**: contraindicated.
- **Patients on other narrow-therapeutic-index CYP3A4 substrates**: class-general concern.
- **Patients tapering immunosuppression**: even low-dose regimens are at risk; induction effect is disproportionate at low levels.

### What we don't know

The onset time of clinically relevant induction varies between patients (reports range from days to weeks). Genetic variation in PXR / CYP3A4 likely modulates individual susceptibility but is not used clinically to stratify risk. Whether any specific low-hyperforin formulation is truly safe in transplant recipients has not been tested and would be unethical to test given the documented harm.

## Recommendation

If you have had an organ transplant and are taking cyclosporine, tacrolimus, or sirolimus, do not take St John's wort under any circumstance. This applies to every product form: capsules, teas, tinctures, combination "mood" supplements that list hypericum as an ingredient. If you are currently taking both, contact your transplant center immediately — not your next routine appointment, but today — and stop the St John's wort. Your cyclosporine levels need to be rechecked quickly. For mood symptoms, your transplant team can discuss alternatives that do not induce CYP3A4.

## Timing advice

Spacing doses does not help. CYP3A4 induction is a transcriptional effect taking days to develop and 1-2 weeks to fully resolve after stopping SJW. There is no timing workaround.

## Severity: danger

Justification: DANGER requires non-reversible harm or emergency-level morbidity. Transplant rejection is both. Multiple published cases across organ types, a clear mechanism, and unanimous transplant-medicine recommendation to contraindicate all converge on DANGER. The evidence is as strong as any supplement-drug interaction in the list, and the consequence (lost graft, potential need for re-transplantation) is among the most severe.
