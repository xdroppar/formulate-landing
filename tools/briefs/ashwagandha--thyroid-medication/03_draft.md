# Stage 3 — Draft

Pair: **ashwagandha** + **thyroid medication**

## Summary

Ashwagandha can raise thyroid hormone levels and stack additively with levothyroxine, risking thyrotoxicosis.

## Details

### Mechanism

Ashwagandha (*Withania somnifera*) appears to stimulate thyroid output, raising circulating T3 and T4 and lowering TSH [moderate, S3]. The effect was most clearly demonstrated in subclinical hypothyroid patients given 600 mg/day root extract for 8 weeks, where TSH fell (p<0.001), T3 rose (p=0.0031), and T4 rose (p=0.0096) versus placebo [moderate, S3]. A separate RCT in bipolar patients found the same direction of effect at 500 mg/day for 8 weeks — free T4 rose by 7%, 12.1%, and 23.7% in the three ashwagandha-treated patients with measurable thyroid changes, while placebo patients generally trended down — though between-group difference was not statistically significant in that underpowered sample [weak, S2]. The Memorial Sloan Kettering monograph notes ashwagandha "may increase thyroxine levels" and cites multiple case reports of associated thyrotoxicosis [moderate, S4]. The biochemical mechanism is not fully established in human literature — authors of the 2022 case report explicitly flagged both direct glandular stimulation and the alternative possibility of exogenous T3/T4 contamination of the supplement itself [weak, S1].

### Evidence quality

The two human RCTs (N=50 subclinical hypothyroid [S3]; N=60 bipolar [S2]) were both short (8 weeks) and single-site. No RCT has directly co-administered ashwagandha with levothyroxine or antithyroid medication, so the interaction claim rests on a mechanism inference plus case-report evidence [weak, S1] [moderate, S4]. A safety RCT in healthy adults at 300 mg twice daily did not find significant thyroid changes, which suggests the effect scales with baseline thyroid state — most pronounced in subclinical hypothyroidism, smaller or absent in euthyroid individuals.

### Clinical relevance

For a patient on stable levothyroxine, adding ashwagandha at 500 mg/day or higher could push free T4 upward by roughly 7–24% over 8 weeks based on the bipolar-population data [weak, S2]. That magnitude is large enough to move a well-titrated patient into a suppressed-TSH or frankly thyrotoxic range. The 2022 case [weak, S1] illustrates what that looks like clinically: a 73-year-old woman with prior hypothyroidism developed supraventricular tachycardia and biochemical thyrotoxicosis (TSH <0.01) after two years of daily ashwagandha; she resolved fully without intervention other than stopping the supplement.

### Dose considerations

Thyroid-stimulating effects are documented at **500 mg/day and 600 mg/day** of standardized root extract [moderate, S2] [moderate, S3]. Below 500 mg/day the effect is not clearly established. Most retail ashwagandha products sold for adaptogen/stress use fall at or above this threshold, so real-world exposure usually puts patients in the studied dose range.

### Populations to watch

- **Patients on levothyroxine or liothyronine**: highest concern — direct additive effect on circulating thyroid hormone.
- **Patients on antithyroid medication (e.g. methimazole, propylthiouracil)**: ashwagandha works in the opposite direction of the medication and may undermine treatment.
- **Patients with prior thyroid disease, even off medication**: the 2022 case [weak, S1] was in a patient who had been off levothyroxine for 2 years.
- **Pregnancy and pediatrics**: not studied; combined with other safety concerns, avoid.

### What we don't know

No human RCT has tested the direct co-administration of ashwagandha with levothyroxine. Dose-response below 500 mg/day is uncertain. Whether supplement contamination with exogenous T3/T4 contributes to case-report presentations is unresolved [weak, S1].

## Recommendation

If you take levothyroxine or any thyroid medication, talk to the clinician who manages your thyroid before starting ashwagandha. If you and your clinician decide to proceed, retest TSH and free T4 within 4–8 weeks of starting, and again if you change dose or brand. Watch for palpitations, unintentional weight loss, heat intolerance, or anxiety — those suggest the combination has pushed you toward hyperthyroidism. Stop ashwagandha and retest if they appear.

## Timing advice

Separating the two medications by hours does **not** mitigate this interaction — the effect is on thyroid output over weeks, not on absorption. Timing-based workarounds do not apply here.

## Severity: warning

Justification: The interaction has a plausible, RCT-demonstrated direction (thyroid stimulation at 500–600 mg/day for 8 weeks) and case-report-level evidence of clinical thyrotoxicosis in vulnerable patients. It does not meet DANGER because (a) all documented cases resolved on discontinuation, (b) no RCT has directly tested co-administration, and (c) the effect is gradual and monitorable. WARNING — meaningful clinical impact, mitigatable with timing/dose/monitoring — is the defensible call. Any upgrade to DANGER requires direct co-administration RCT evidence or documented non-reversible harm.
