# Stage 3 — Draft

Pair: **SSRI** + **St John's wort**

## Summary

St John's wort plus any SSRI risks serotonin syndrome — a potentially fatal state. Do not combine.

## Details

### Mechanism

St John's wort (*Hypericum perforatum*) has antidepressant activity in its own right, acting as a weak monoamine-reuptake inhibitor and modulating serotonin receptors [moderate, S1]. When taken alongside an SSRI, the two agents converge on the same pathway — both raise synaptic serotonin — producing additive central serotonergic activity [moderate, S1]. This is a pharmacodynamic synergy, not just a pharmacokinetic metabolic change. That distinction matters: low-hyperforin SJW products cause fewer CYP3A4-mediated drug-metabolism interactions, but their serotonergic effect on the SSRI pathway is not eliminated [moderate, S2]. There is no hyperforin-content threshold below which the combination becomes safe.

### Evidence quality

No RCT has deliberately co-administered SJW with an SSRI — and it would be unethical to run one given the documented harm signal [weak, S1]. The evidence base is therefore a combination of mechanism (well-established monoamine activity of SJW constituents) plus spontaneous adverse-event reports. Published case reports describe serotonin syndrome across multiple SSRIs — sertraline and paroxetine are most often named, with SSRI doses in the standard therapeutic range (20–75 mg/day) and SJW doses of 600–900 mg/day standardized extract [moderate, S3]. Three independent major reviews (Borrelli 2007, Nicolussi 2020, StatPearls 2024) all converge on the same recommendation: do not combine [moderate, S1] [moderate, S2] [moderate, S3].

### Clinical relevance

Serotonin syndrome ranges from mild (shivering, diaphoresis, mild tremor) to life-threatening (hyperthermia >41.1 °C, seizures, rhabdomyolysis, disseminated intravascular coagulation, death). Signs typically include at least three of: confusion, agitation, hyperreflexia, clonus, shivering or tremor, nausea, diarrhea, incoordination, fever, flushing [moderate, S1]. Onset can be within hours of adding the second serotonergic agent, and the severity correlates with the cumulative serotonergic load — so a fully-titrated SSRI plus a standard SJW dose carries meaningful clinical risk, not just a theoretical one.

### Dose considerations

No safe dose of SJW has been established for co-administration with any SSRI [moderate, S3]. Case reports span the entire range of commonly-sold SJW doses (600–900 mg/day) and standard therapeutic SSRI doses. Reducing either agent's dose does not reliably prevent the syndrome.

### Populations to watch

- **Any patient on any SSRI**: sertraline, paroxetine, fluoxetine, citalopram, escitalopram, fluvoxamine — all carry the interaction.
- **Patients on other serotonergic agents combined with SJW**: tramadol, MAOIs, triptans, linezolid, trazodone, MDMA — these compound the risk and any combination with SJW is contraindicated, not just the SSRI case.
- **Patients switching from SJW to an SSRI** should have a washout period before escalating the SSRI dose; the timeline is not precisely established but 1–2 weeks mirrors clinical practice for SSRI-to-SSRI switches.
- **Pregnancy, pediatrics, elderly**: not studied and not appropriate to test.

### What we don't know

We do not have RCT-grade effect sizes for serotonin syndrome risk. We do not have a validated dose-response curve. We do not know whether specific SSRIs carry different relative risks with SJW — sertraline and paroxetine are over-represented in reports but this may reflect prescribing prevalence, not intrinsic risk.

## Recommendation

Do not take St John's wort while on any SSRI. If you are currently on both, stop the St John's wort and contact your prescriber before your next SSRI dose. If you want to try St John's wort for mood and are on an SSRI, discuss an SSRI taper with your prescriber first; do not self-manage a switch. If you experience shivering, tremor, agitation, racing heart, or high fever after starting the combination, stop both agents and seek emergency medical evaluation — serotonin syndrome requires treatment, not a wait-and-see approach.

## Timing advice

Spacing doses does not mitigate this interaction. Both agents act on synaptic serotonin continuously once absorbed, and SSRIs reach steady state over 1–5 weeks. There is no timing-based workaround.

## Severity: danger

Justification: DANGER applies when co-administration carries risk of non-reversible harm or emergency presentation with significant morbidity. Serotonin syndrome meets both bars — documented fatalities in the broader serotonergic-combination literature, emergency presentations (hyperthermia, seizures, rhabdomyolysis), and no effective mitigation short of discontinuation. Three independent major references endorse "do not combine." The evidence is mechanism-dense even without RCT confirmation (which would be unethical). This is the archetypal DANGER pair.
